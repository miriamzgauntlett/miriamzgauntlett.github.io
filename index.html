about

I am a postdoctoral researcher in observational seismology. My research uses seismic imaging to understand the Earth's interior structure—specifically the crust and the upper mantle.

I live and work on Ngunnawal and Ngambri country at the Australian National University.

research

current

Currently, I am working on the WA Array project with Dr Caroline Eakin, a continental-wide array of seismometers aiming to provide unprecedented insights into the structure of Western Australia's crust and mantle. Western Australia is one of the oldest continental regions in the world, with a complex, lengthy geological history. Rigid continental plates record their deformation history in large-scale rock fabric that alters the directional dependence of seismic wave speed: a property known as seismic anisotropy. Current deformation occurring beneath plates within the mantle also contributes to seismic anisotropy. I am using shear-wave splitting to measure seismic anisotropy in the south-western area of Western Australia. Preliminary results emphasise the complexity of the Earth’s interior beneath this region, with our anisotropy results likely reflecting past deformation events fossilised into the lithosphere.

phd

masters

During my masters at University College London, I worked with Ana Pereira on a project that investigated the space-time charcteristics of large-magnitude, deep earthquakes. These mysterious events occur at depths where the ordinary earthquake mechanism of brittle failure should be inhibited, as the pressures and temperatures are too high. In contrast to their shallow counterparts, the space-time characteristics determined for deep earthquakes are often inconsistent, non-unique and poorly constrained. Moreover, deep events have either been understudied or not studied at all due to the difficulty of inverting teleseismic data to determine their source parameters. However, accurate estimates of the space-time characteristics of deep earthquakes is essential to understanding the rupture mechanisms that govern these mysterious events. I determined the source parameters of the largest magnitude (Mw > 7.5), deepest (depth > 300 km) earthquakes in the time period 2000–2020, in order to gain an insight into the rupture physics of deep seismicity.
